In March of 2025, I left my job as a Senior Program Officer at the National Endowment for the Humanities. 

My ability to walk away from what had long been a great job was inspired in part by my brilliant colleague Becca Quon. In 2024, Becca wrote [a blog post]((https://beccaquon.com/projects/sabbatical/)) about her decision to leave her position for a self-funded sabbatical. I thought about the post often, and promised myself that if I ever left NEH, I would do the same.

A year has passed, and it's time for me to fulfill that commitment. Here are my reflection on how and why I left NEH, what I did next, and where I'm at now.

# Why I left NEH
When I read Becca's blog post for the first time, I thought that one day I too would feel like I had accomplished everything I wanted at NEH, and that it was time for a change. When that happened, I hoped I would have the courage to leave.

You could say that after five years at NEH, I had hit that point. I was seven years out from my PhD. In a different world, I would be going up for tenure and planning for a sabbatical. I had a book to write and big dreams for myself and I was very, very tired. 

The context is a little different than that, though, because the 2024 presidential election changed things dramatically. The minute that the Trump administration took office, I stopped being able to do my job. Instead, I was spending most of my time trying to figure out how to respond to blatantly discriminatory attempts to cancel so-called DEIA grants, fielding labor concerns from my colleagues, and trying to figure out how to organize against a Reduction in Force (federal layoffs). 

I left my job at the end of March. Within three months, over 1400 awards would be terminated. [(In a surprise to no one, this was recently found to be unconstitutional.)](https://www.nytimes.com/2026/05/07/arts/neh-grants-lawsuit-doge.html) Some seventy percent of union members would be laid off. The grant program that I had spent two years designing and launching would be cancelled. The Office of Data and Evaluation, which I spent three years helping to establish, would be shut down. 

All of this is to say that my departure was not one of personal or professional triumph. I left broken-hearted, traumatized, and feeling really guilty about the people I had left behind.

# What It Took To Leave
Because I left of my own volition, and was not RIFed, I did not receive any kind of severance pay or unemployment benefits. I also do not have a domestic partner, which means I do not have a second income or source of health insurance.

To be able to afford time off, I depended entirely on my own savings, which was an incredible privilege. Like Becca, I worked with a financial planner. [Kaylie, my financial planner,](https://butchonabudget.com/) is currently not taking clients. [Here's the link to Becca's](https://www.moneypositive.com/). I literally cannot recommend this enough. It was life changing.

Kaylie and I determined that my expenses were about $5,500 per month. To take six months off, I would need $33,000. I decided to set aside $40,000 so that I could live comfortably during this time.

My major expenses included:
* Rent and utilities
* Marketplace health insurance, rental insurance, and car insurance
* Personal and pet expenses like groceries, cat food, vet
* Donations and subscriptions
* Luxuries like eating out, going to shows, and traveling

In my mind, at the end of six months, I would have a job again, and so although it felt terrifying, it would be okay to spend all this money. As you will see, this was not the case. Fortunately, I had enough savings to float me for a few more months. My parents gifted me $15,000 unexpectedly, and I was able to pick up a summer class at the local university, which paid $6,000 before taxes. Finally, I was able to cut my expenses down to about $4,500 per month. This is because I live in Tennessee and, another great privilege, I do not have any debt.

# Six-Month Sabbatical
My vision for my time off was to take six months of sabbatical. My goals for this time were to recover from burnout, finish writing my book, and figure out what I wanted to do next.

I knew in theory that burnout was a real thing, but I did not actually believe it until I tried to take a sabbatical. My plan was to take two months to rest and then spend four months writing and preparing to go back to work. 

The time I took to rest was incredible. I jumped in my car and spent a month on the road, visiting friends and hiking solo in our public lands. I met up with beloved colleagues, friends, and family in Sedona, Flagstaff, Denver, Minneapolis, and Bloomington. I soaked alone in remote hot springs and camped next to bison and drove through blizzards and ate a donut at the wildlife refuge in Roswell, New Mexico, where I worked in 2003.

I went back home just in time for the birth of my favorite and only niece, and then travelled again. I spent a month in Mexico City at a friend's apartment, visiting museums and drinking coffee. I spent a month house sitting for my parents in New England, walking in some of my favorite forests. I went on a lot of cute dates and met some incredible people.

Writing the book was harder. In fact, I made very little progress on my writing goals during those six months. This is what I mean when I say that burnout is real. I spent miserable, tortured hours trying to force myself to work. But nothing came. I have enough experience as a writer to know that this is not something you can demand. But I felt nauseaus with fear that I would go back to work without having finished this project.

Figuring out what I wanted to do next was also very difficult. Or rather, it was complicated. 

Immediately, I knew that I wanted to reinvest in my community. When I finally stopped traveling in September, I decided to start a weekly coffee house, a free third space where people could gather and work and be in community. I partnered with my brother and sister-in-law on this project, which is maybe the best thing I have ever done. It is still operating, and is an incredible source of joy and comfort for me. Unfortunately, because it is free, it is by definition not a source of income.

An opportunity also arose when three former NEH colleagues invited me to start a business together. We all had shared values and commitments to ensuring that the cultural sector would continue to thrive during difficult times. In July of 2025, I flew to Washington, DC for a business retreat. We launched [Sidra Collaborative](https://sidracollaborative.com/) in September, and it has been an extraoardinary opportunity to work with funders and cultural organizations to build a better future for arts, culture, and the humanities.

# And Then What Happened
My six month sabbatical ended in October of 2025. This is where things got weird.

Money immediately became a huge source of stress. I wanted to invest in a real future with Sidra, and we were starting to get contract work, but not enough to pay a living wage. In the meantime, I conducted informational interviews with colleagues I admired who were doing interesting or motivating work. I met with the small business advisory office at my local community college, a free service that provides information on how to be self-employed. I started trying to pick up additional sources of income, and was able to do some [grant writing and developmental editing](https://halperta.com/categories/mentorship/). This helped to stretch out my remaining savings. I also applied to full-time jobs, but I was not ready to take a position that didn't feel like a great fit. This was made infinitely more stressful by the fact that public discourse around the job market right now says that *everything is bad.*

Writing immediately became easy. Six months turns out to have been how much time I needed to remember what it felt like to be energized around work. I finished the majority of my manuscript and started soliciting reader feedback and looking for publishers. This has been exciting and motivating and terrifying. This is not my first book (I was a ghostwriter many years ago), but it is the first manuscript that would be published under my own name. It turns out publishing your own book requires a lot of confidence and determination, especially when public discourse around publishing says that *everything is bad.* 

The second six months of my sabbatical year are harder to describe than the first. I worked a lot, largely on short-term contracts that were really great but not quite enough money to live on. I deepened my commitment to friends and family and romantic partners. I woke up in the middle of the night in a panic because I didn't know how I would pay my expenses. I went on hikes and visited friends and read books and baked and volunteered. I began to feel like I had a vision for the future, for myself and my career and my profession. I felt ready to take on leadership roles again, ones that would allow me to invest more deeply in work I believe in.

# Where I Am Now
When Becca published a post about her sabbatical year, she was able to announce that she had found a new job, one that was a great fit for her values and skills. I had imagined that my post would look similar. It does not.

Fourteen months after I left NEH, I am still in some kind of limbo. I feel immense clarity around myself, my work, and my professional purpose. I feel immense gratitude for my relationships, my home, and the various paid and unpaid projects that I am part of. 

But these are hard times. There is a lot that is scary, both globally and locally. I've been reading [Search Work](https://orbooks.com/catalog/search-work/) and feeling grateful to recognize how I fit into a much larger story of social and economic transformation. I've been rereading adrienne maree brown and reminding myself that god is change. I've been working hard to believe that there is a future for me and for the work I believe in, even when it's hard to see.

If I had it to do over again, I might have preferred the option to leave my job later and under different circumstances. I would have liked to see my projects through. I would have liked to leave my workplace better than I found it.

But I would leave again, when it was time, even if I still don't know exactly how it will all turn out. I would leave because I remain committed to the belief that time away from work is something all of us need and deserve. If leaving a job is something you are thinking about, I hope this is encouraging, even now.
